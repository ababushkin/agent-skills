## Item-by-item breakdown

### 1. Makita 18V Drill — LIST IT

**Why it sells:** Makita 18V is one of the most popular tool platforms in Australia. Tradies and
DIYers actively seek used Makita gear because batteries are expensive new (~$120–$180 each
from Bunnings) and the platform is interchangeable. A drill with two batteries and a charger
is a complete kit — that's the key selling point.

**Pricing:**
- Makita 18V drill body only: $60–$90
- Each 18V battery new: ~$130–$160
- Two batteries + charger new: $250–$300 combined
- Used kit with drill, 2 batteries, charger, good condition: **$120–$160 is realistic and
  will move quickly.** Price at $150 and accept $120. If you have the original case, bump
  the ask to $160–$170.

**Tips:**
- Photograph the drill, both batteries, charger, and any case separately and as a group.
- State the battery voltage (18V) and model number if you can find it (check the base of
  the drill — e.g. DDF482, DHP482).
- Note condition of the battery cells — "both batteries hold charge, tested" is worth stating.
- List on both Facebook Marketplace and Gumtree; tools sell faster on FB Marketplace in
  most Australian metro areas.

### 2. IKEA Billy Bookcase (white, 3-shelf) — LIST IT

**Why it sells:** Billy is the best-selling bookcase in Australia and IKEA has a cult following
on the secondhand market. Buyers know exactly what they're getting and don't need to inspect
closely. A minor chip on one corner is normal wear — disclose it and it won't kill the sale.

**Pricing:**
- New Billy 3-shelf (80x202x28): ~$119 from IKEA
- Used, good condition: $50–$70
- Used with minor chip: **$30–$45** is the right bracket. Price at $40, firm.
- Assembled or flat-pack matters: if still assembled, price higher ($40–$45) because the
  buyer saves effort. If you need to disassemble for transport, price at $30.

**Tips:**
- Disclose the chip clearly with a photo — buyers will trust you more for it and you avoid
  time-wasters asking "any damage?"
- State dimensions (80cm wide × 202cm tall × 28cm deep is standard Billy).
- "Buyer must disassemble / bring help" or "I can help load" — be clear about this.
- Local pickup only; no courier for flatpack.

### 3. Box of ~50 Mixed DVDs — MARGINAL, LIST AS BUNDLE

**Why it's borderline:** Individual DVDs are nearly worthless on the secondhand market in
Australia — op shops barely accept them, and $1 each is optimistic. However, a bulk lot of
50 does attract buyers: families with young kids, people without reliable streaming, collectors
who want specific titles, or op shops/libraries restocking.

**Pricing:**
- $10–$20 for the entire box, buyer collects.
- Do not individually price or list separately — the effort isn't worth it.
- If you have any box sets, TV series collections, or Blu-rays mixed in, pull those out and
  mention them specifically in the listing title — they lift perceived value.

**Decision rule:** If you'd rather not deal with a $15 transaction, just donate to an op shop
(Vinnies, Salvos) or put them out on the nature strip with a "FREE — take one or all" sign.
They will be gone within hours. That is the zero-effort correct answer for most people.

**If you do list:**
- Title: "Box of 50 DVDs — mixed titles — $15 the lot"
- Include a photo of the spread of covers so buyers can see what's in there.
- Price firm and state "no holds, first cash takes."

---

### 4. CRT Monitor 19" — DO NOT LIST

**Why it's not worth it:** CRT monitors have effectively zero resale value in Australia in
2025. The secondhand electronics market is flooded with flat-panel monitors; no one needs
the weight, desk space, or power draw of a CRT. E-waste collectors like Officeworks
(FreedomRecycle) and local council e-waste drop-off points take them for free. You will
not sell this on Marketplace — you'll get lowball offers from nobody, or inquiries from
people who ghost when they see the pickup hassle.

**What to do instead:**
- Check your local council's e-waste collection schedule or drop-off points
  (most Australian councils run these quarterly or permanently).
- Officeworks accepts e-waste in-store including monitors — no purchase necessary.
- TechCollect (techcollect.com.au) has drop-off locations around Australia.

Do not put it on the nature strip — CRTs contain lead and can't go to landfill.
