# Selling a Juicer with a Damaged Power Cord — Australian Context

## Short answer: No, you should not sell it as-is

Selling a secondhand appliance with a nicked power cord in Australia is not okay — not for safety reasons alone, but also because it likely exposes you to legal liability. Here's a full breakdown.

---

## Why this matters more than it might seem

A nick in the insulation near the plug is not a cosmetic issue. That's the highest-risk location on a cord — close to where the plug generates heat and mechanical stress. Exposed or compromised insulation near the plug can cause:

- Electric shock to the user
- Short circuits
- Fire risk (the juicer has a motor, so there's significant current draw)

The fact that the juicer "works fine" doesn't reduce the hazard — the fault is in the power delivery, not the appliance function.

---

## Legal position in Australia

**Australian Consumer Law (ACL)** applies to private sales of goods that have safety defects, not just retail. If you sell an item knowing it has a defect that poses a safety risk, and someone is harmed, you can be held liable.

**Product safety regulations** under the ACL mean that electrical goods with damaged insulation are considered unsafe. The ACCC takes electrical safety seriously — damaged cords on appliances are a known fire and electrocution hazard category.

**Platform rules**: Major Australian resale platforms also prohibit listing items with known electrical faults:
- Facebook Marketplace: prohibits hazardous items; damaged electrical goods fall under this
- Gumtree: similar restrictions
- eBay Australia: prohibits items that don't meet Australian safety standards

Even if you disclose the nick in the listing, disclosure doesn't eliminate your liability if someone is injured — and most buyers won't fully appreciate the risk.

---

## Your actual options

### Option 1: Repair it first, then sell (recommended)

A power cord replacement or a proper cord repair by a licensed electrician is usually inexpensive — often $30–80 for a simple appliance cord repair. Once repaired properly, the juicer becomes fully sellable and you'll get a better price anyway (a working juicer with no visible faults vs. one with a noted defect).

Do not use electrical tape as a fix if you're planning to sell. That's not an adequate repair for insulation damage near a plug.

### Option 2: Sell for parts only — with full disclosure

You could list it explicitly as "for parts only — damaged power cord, not safe for use" at a much lower price. Some buyers (electronics hobbyists, repair people) will take it. This only works if:
- You make the fault completely explicit in the listing
- You price it accordingly (expect well under $10)
- You accept that the buyer understands it's not operational-safe

This is a grey area legally, and the safest path is Option 1.

### Option 3: Dispose of it responsibly

If repair isn't worth it for you, e-waste drop-off is the right move. Breville appliances can be disposed of via:
- **TechCollect** (free e-waste drop-off): techcollect.com.au — find a drop-off point near you
- **Local council e-waste** events or permanent drop-off facilities
- Some Officeworks and other retailers accept small appliance e-waste

Breville also has a recycling program — worth checking their website.

## Summary

| Option | Safe to sell? | Legal? | Practical? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sell as-is without disclosure | No | No | No |
| Sell as-is with disclosure | No | Risky | Marginally |
| Repair cord, then sell | Yes | Yes | Best outcome |
| Sell as parts-only, fully disclosed | Partial | Risky | Low value |
| E-waste disposal | N/A | Yes | Clean exit |

**Recommendation**: Get the cord repaired by a licensed electrician or an appliance repair shop. It's a small cost, makes the item fully sellable, gets you a fair price, and keeps you on the right side of Australian consumer law and your own conscience.
