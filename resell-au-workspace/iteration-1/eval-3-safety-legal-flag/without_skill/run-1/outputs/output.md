## IMPORTANT: Car Seat Safety Warning

Before listing the Britax car seat, please read this carefully.

**Australian Road Rules and child safety authorities strongly advise against buying or selling second-hand car seats** unless the full history of the seat is known with certainty. Here is why this matters for you as the seller:

- A car seat that has been in any crash — even a minor one — must be replaced. Internal damage is invisible to the eye.
- Australian state road authorities (e.g. Transport for NSW, VicRoads, TMR Queensland) and ACCC guidance align with the recommendation that used car seats should only change hands between people who know each other and can vouch for the full history of the seat.
- Britax themselves state that seats involved in a crash should not be reused.
- Selling a seat with an unknown or undisclosed crash history to a stranger, where it is then used by a child, creates a serious safety and potential legal liability risk.

**However — if you can genuinely confirm the following, listing it is reasonable:**
1. The seat has never been in a motor vehicle accident (even a minor one).
2. It is within its expiry date (check the sticker on the seat — most Britax seats expire 8–10 years from manufacture date).
3. All original parts, harness straps, buckles, and the instruction manual are present and undamaged.
4. It has not been recalled (check the ACCC Product Safety Australia recall database: www.productsafety.gov.au).

**If all of the above are true, you can list it with those assurances clearly stated in the ad.** Many families sell seats between known contacts for this reason. On platforms like Facebook Marketplace or Gumtree, being upfront about the history actually helps your listing — buyers of second-hand car seats look specifically for sellers who can confirm no-crash history.

If you are unsure about any of the above, the right call is to dispose of the seat responsibly rather than sell it. Many council hard waste collections and some Britax retailers accept old seats.

## Pricing Guidance (AUD, based on typical AU secondhand market)

### Bell Mountain Bike Helmet
- New retail: typically $60–$150 depending on the Bell model.
- Secondhand, worn only a few times, excellent condition: **$30–$60** is realistic.
- If it's a higher-end Bell model (e.g. Super, Sixer, Sanction), you can push toward $60–$80.
- Tip: include the model name in your listing — buyers search by model.

### Britax Child Car Seat
- New retail: Britax seats range from $200 (basic convertible) to $700+ (premium models like the Platinum or B-Safe).
- Secondhand, excellent condition, verified no-crash history: typically **50–60% of new retail** is achievable for a well-known Britax model in great shape.
- As a rough guide: **$80–$180** depending on the specific model and age.
- Listing below $100 moves it fast; $120–$150 is fair if it's a newer/premium model in genuinely excellent condition.

## Quick Tips for Both Listings

- **Photos matter most.** Take 6–8 clear photos in good natural light: all angles, close-ups of buckles/straps, any labels/stickers, and the overall condition. For the helmet, show the inside foam and the chin strap.
- **Include dimensions/size.** Buyers can't try things on — make it easy for them.
- **Be responsive.** Listings with fast replies get offers; slow replies lose them.
- **For the car seat:** a photo of the manufacture/expiry sticker builds trust immediately. Include it.
- **Price to sell.** If you get no enquiries in a week, drop by $10 and re-list as "new listing."
